INTRODUCTION  
Breast  cancer  is  the  most  frequently  diagnosed  cancer  and  the  second  leading  cause  of  cancer  death  among  
women
 
in
 
the
 
United
 
States.27
 Significant  racial  disparities  persist  in  breast  cancer  outcomes:  African  American  
(AA)
 
women
 
experience
 
approximately
 
40%
 
higher
 
breast
 
cancer
 
mortality
 
than
 
non-Hispanic
 
White
 
(NHW)
 
women
 
and
 
are
 
more
 
likely
 
to
 
be
 
diagnosed
 
with
 
aggressive,
 
high-grade
 
tumors
 
at
 
younger
 
ages.28,29
 
Environmental
 
exposures
 
including
 
endocrine-disrupting
 
chemicals
 
and
 
heavy
 
metals
 
are
 
increasingly
 
recognized
 
as
 
contributors
 
to
 
these
 
disparities,
 
yet
 
the
 
specific
 
mechanisms
 
by
 
which
 
racial
 
background
 
modifies
 
vulnerability
 
to
 
environmental
 
carcinogens
 
remain
 
incompletely
 
characterized.30
 
Arsenic  is  a  Group  1  human  carcinogen  classified  by  the  International  Agency  for  Research  on  Cancer  and  an  
established
 
environmental
 
carcinogen
 
with
 
documented
 
associations
 
with
 
breast
 
cancer
 
risk,
 
particularly
 
for
 
premenopausal
 
women
 
and
 
with
 
evidence
 
for
 
racial
 
variation
 
in
 
tissue-level
 
sensitivity.31
 In  the  United  States,  
arsenic
 
contamination
 
of
 
drinking
 
water
 
affects
 
millions
 
of
 
people,
 
with
 
particularly
 
high
 
levels
 
documented
 
in
 
communities
 
across
 
South
 
Florida
 
and
 
other
 
Southern
 
states
 
that
 
also
 
carry
 
elevated
 
rates
 
of
 
advanced
 
cancer
 
diagnosis.4
 Arsenic  promotes  carcinogenesis  through  multiple  mechanisms  including  oxidative  stress,  
epigenetic
 
dysregulation,
 
and
 
-
 
critically
 
-
 
inhibition
 
of
 
DNA
 
repair.32,33
 Unlike  classical  mutagens  that  directly  
cause
 
point
 
mutations,
 
arsenic
 
acts
 
primarily
 
as
 
a
 
co-carcinogen
 
by
 
impairing
 
the
 
capacity
 
of
 
cells
 
to
 
remove
 
DNA
 
damage
 
caused
 
by
 
other
 
environmental
 
insults,
 
amplifying
 
their
 
mutagenic
 
effect.34
 
Nucleotide  excision  repair  (NER)  is  the  primary  DNA  repair  pathway  responsible  for  removing  a  broad  
spectrum
 
of
 
helix-distorting
 
lesions,
 
including
 
bulky
 
adducts
 
induced
 
by
 
polycyclic
 
aromatic
 
hydrocarbons,
 
UV
 
radiation,
 
and
 
arsenic-associated
 
oxidative
 
damage.35,36
 NER  operates  through  two  sub-pathways:  
global-genome
 
NER
 
(GG-NER),
 
which
 
surveys
 
the
 
entire
 
genome
 
for
 
lesions,
 
and
 
transcription-coupled
 
NER
 
(TC-NER),
 
which
 
preferentially
 
repairs
 
damage
 
in
 
actively
 
transcribed
 
genes.
 
Both
 
converge
 
on
 
a
 
common
 
incision
 
machinery
 
involving
 
the
 
ERCC1-XPF
 
endonuclease
 
complex,
 
which
 
executes
 
the
 
critical
 
5′
 
incision
 
step.40,41
 Arsenic  inhibits  NER  through  multiple  mechanisms:  it  reduces  expression  of  the  damage  recognition  
factor
 
XPC,
 
displaces
 
zinc
 
from
 
the
 
zinc
 
finger
 
domains
 
of
 
NER
 
proteins
 
XPA
 
and
 
PARP-1,
 
and
 
suppresses
 
p53-mediated
 
transcriptional
 
activation
 
of
 
NER
 
genes.34,35,36
 The  consequence  is  that  cells  co-exposed  to  
arsenic
 
and
 
other
 
carcinogens
 
accumulate
 
unrepaired
 
NER
 
substrates
 
at
 
a
 
higher
 
rate,
 
potentiating
 
carcinogenesis.
 
Importantly,
 
prior
 
work
 
characterizing
 
arsenic-NER
 
interactions
 
has
 
been
 
conducted
 
in
 
race-indeterminate
 
bulk
 
cell
 
populations;
 
whether
 
the
 
magnitude
 
or
 
context-dependence
 
of
 
arsenic-induced
 
NER
 
impairment
 
differs
 
by
 
racial
 
background
 
has
 
not
 
been
 
investigated.
 
Cancer  stem  cells  (CSCs)  represent  a  self-renewing,  tumor-initiating  subpopulation  implicated  in  breast  cancer  
initiation,
 
therapy
 
resistance,
 
and
 
recurrence.6,7
 In  breast  tissue-derived  cell  systems,  CSCs  are  identified  by  the  
surface
 
phenotype
 
CD24−/CD49f+/CD44+:
 
loss
 
of
 
the
 
differentiation
 
marker
 
CD24,
 
retention
 
of
 
the
 
mammary
 
progenitor

CD49f
 
(integrin
 
α6),
 
and
 
co-expression
 
of
 
the
 
adhesion
 
receptor
 
CD44.6,7
 This  marker

isolates
 
a
 
population
 
with
 
enhanced
 
self-renewal
 
capacity,
 
elevated
 
DNA
 
repair
 
programs,
 
and
 
resistance
 
to
 
genotoxic
 
stress
 
-
 
properties
 
that
 
make
 
CSCs
 
an
 
especially
 
relevant
 
cellular
 
compartment
 
for
 
studying
 
the
 
long-term
 
consequences
 
of
 
arsenic
 
exposure.
 
Despite
 
the
 
established
 
importance
 
of
 
CSCs
 
in
 
breast
 
carcinogenesis,
 
the
 
transcriptional
 
biology
 
of
 
CSCs
 
derived
 
from
 
racially
 
distinct
 
breast
 
tissue-derived
 
cell
 
populations,
 
and
 
the
 
impact
 
of
 
arsenic
 
on
 
these
 
differences,
 
has
 
not
 
been
 
characterized.
 
This  study  addresses  the  intersection  of  these  three  underexplored  areas  -  racial  biology,  arsenic-induced  DNA  
repair
 
impairment,
 
and
 
cancer
 
stem
 
cell
 
transcriptomics
 
-
 
using
 
a
 
genome-wide
 
RNA
 
sequencing
 
approach
 
with
 
a
 
specific
 
design
 
to
 
detect
 
accumulated
 
small
 
changes
 
in
 
NER
 
and
 
DNA
 
repair
 
pathway
 
gene
 
expression
 
rather
